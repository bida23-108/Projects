Introduction

Abstract

The introduction of Starlink in Botswana in 2024 marked a significant shift in the market. Leveraging Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellite technology, Starlink offers high-speed broadband services without the need for extensive terrestrial infrastructure. This enables it to reach geographically dispersed populations, including those in rural and remote areas historically underserved by conventional fixed and mobile broadband providers.

This study assesses and evaluates the impact of Starlink's entry into Botswana's communications market and its adherence to licensing conditions.

Background

Botswana's broadband industry has undergone significant changes over the last decade. The fixed broadband sector comprises both infrastructure-based ISPs and MNOs offering fixed broadband via LTE, 5G, radio, and fibre services. Starlink's entry introduced a fundamentally new technology layer — satellite broadband — that operates independently of all existing terrestrial infrastructure.

Problem Statement

While all licensees are subject to regulatory requirements, operators have raised concerns regarding perceived regulatory asymmetry. MNOs and ISPs comply with strict KYC obligations, citizen employment requirements, and local infrastructure investment mandates. Operators contend that Starlink has not been held to the same standards, creating an uneven competitive playing field.

Study Objectives

  • Analyse fixed broadband subscription trends across MNOs, ISPs, and Starlink; assess market share changes
  • Evaluate the impact of Starlink's entry on operator revenues across the market
  • Assess changes in employment levels including evidence of job creation or displacement
  • Examine capital expenditure trends to determine whether Starlink's presence has influenced infrastructure rollout decisions
  • Evaluate competitive responses of existing operators — pricing strategies, product offerings, and service innovation
  • Assess Starlink's compliance with its licensing conditions, including alignment between approved terms and actual market practices

Licensing & Financial Obligations

Starlink was issued its SAP licence on 14 May 2024 and NFP licence on 10 January 2025, both on a provisional basis. Three earth stations were established in Gaborone, Lobatse, and Francistown as part of mandatory infrastructure investment obligations. All three facilities are fully operational.

⚠ Turnover Levy — Non-Payment

While Starlink has paid annual licence fees for SAP and NFP licences since 2024, no invoices have been issued and consequently no payments made in respect of BOCRA turnover levies — a mandatory requirement under Starlink's licence. Starlink's financial year ended in December 2025 and BOCRA is in the process of determining the applicable levy amount.

⚠ UASF Levy — Outstanding

The 2025 UASF levy contribution framework categorises Starlink as a mandatory contributor based on annual turnover from regulated services. Together with other new contributors, Starlink has been notified of this obligation, but no payments have been received.

Network Deployment

Starlink LEO Constellation — 550 km altitude, ~27,000 km/h orbital speed
Ground Station Satellite Dish — Starlink earth stations at Gaborone, Lobatse & Francistown

LEO Network Infrastructure

  • Three fully operational earth stations: Gaborone (primary gateway), Lobatse, and Francistown
  • LEO orbit altitude ~550 km provides near-national coverage, including geographically hard-to-reach areas of the Kalahari and Okavango
  • Satellites orbit at ~27,000 km/h with 53° inclination — each pass over Botswana lasts 8–12 minutes
  • Low latency of 25–60ms (typical real-world range) — competitive with many terrestrial broadband services
  • Supports homes, businesses, schools, clinics, enterprises, and government facilities across the full national territory

Traffic Routing & BINX Non-Compliance

  • Starlink does not peer with BINX (Botswana Internet Exchange). All traffic is routed internationally — via Nigeria or South Africa — bypassing domestic internet exchange infrastructure
  • This creates a measurable economic externality: local internet traffic that could be exchanged domestically costs other operators more by forcing international routing
  • In late 2025, BOCRA received a Starlink application for spectrum in the Mobile Satellite Services (MSS) band — under review
  • Starlink is preparing to launch Very Low Earth Orbit (VLEO) satellites enabling voice services — potentially competing directly with MNO voice revenues if MSS licence is granted
Spectrum Pricing Asymmetry

BOCRA's existing spectrum pricing structure sets IMT (terrestrial mobile) licences significantly higher than satellite licences. Domestic operators face higher input costs for equivalent coverage capabilities — a structural competitive disadvantage that BOCRA is in the process of reviewing.

Data Sovereignty & Privacy

⚠ Data Protection Act 2024 — Compliance Gap

Section 74 of the 2024 Data Protection Act requires all personal data collected in Botswana to be kept locally, and any cross-border data transfer to be subject to adequacy requirements. Starlink's architecture routes all user data through satellite links to ground stations and then overseas before reaching the public internet — a design that may not satisfy domestic data localisation obligations.

Generic Global Privacy Policy — Insufficient

Starlink has submitted a generic Global Privacy Policy to BOCRA rather than a Botswana-specific policy as required. KYC obligations — mandatory for all licensed operators — remain only partially met. Unlike SIM-card based MNO services, Starlink's dish-based service model does not inherently capture subscriber identity at point of activation without additional compliance controls mandated by BOCRA.

Compliance with lawful interception, cybersecurity incident reporting, and local data storage obligations must be codified as licence conditions prior to any long-term licence being issued.

Key Market Finding — Pre vs Post Starlink Entry

Pre-Starlink (July 2023 – Sep 2024): The market was characterised by stable, modest growth primarily by MNOs. Orange recorded consistent growth from 197,423 (Jan–Mar 2024) to 223,412 (Jul–Sep 2024). Mascom grew significantly from 164,899 to 237,772 in the same period. BTC experienced gradual decline, while all ISPs maintained broadly stable subscriber bases.

Post-Starlink (Oct 2024 – Mar 2026): Starlink accumulated 3,085 subscribers in its first quarter, reaching 19,958 by Q1 2026 — a 547% increase. The total market grew 17.1%, but the composition shifted materially: Orange and Mascom collectively hold 84.6% of subscriptions while all five independent ISPs are now in sustained decline.

Revenue Displacement — Quantified

BoFiNet's revenue market share dropped from 7.9% to 6.0% — a structural substitution effect as Starlink utilises its own LEO constellation for backhaul, bypassing the traditional national backbone. Combined small ISP revenue (Ngami Net + Abaricom + GcSat + Paratus) declined from approximately BWP 32.1M (Q3 2024) to BWP 26.9M (Q4 2025). Over the same period, Starlink grew from zero to BWP 23.5M — a near-direct revenue transfer within the ISP segment. The market is not shrinking; it is redistributing.

CAPEX Insight — Investment Slowdown Risk

In Q4 2025, the sector saw a general CAPEX decline. Mascom reduced capital spending by 20.7% to BWP 307.1M, while Orange reduced by 28.7% to BWP 132.5M. This is consistent with operators adopting a more cautious posture in response to competitive uncertainty from satellite broadband — a pattern with concerning long-term implications for Botswana's broadband infrastructure quality and geographic coverage expansion.

Starlink Employment Anomaly

Starlink maintains only 1 recorded local employee across all observed periods while generating BWP 27.4M per quarter in revenue. By contrast, domestic operators collectively employ hundreds of Batswana across technical, retail, and customer service roles. Small ISPs are now adjusting employment downward — GcSat reduced to 9 staff, Ngami Net to 12 — reflecting competitive pressure. Abaricom paradoxically grew from 78 to 108 staff despite revenue declining 34%.

Competitive Responses — Pricing Adjustments

Operators across the market have been forced to adjust their pricing in direct response to Starlink's entry. One operator described being "forced to drop rates aggressively" to retain subscribers. Another doubled bandwidth on all shared packages without price increases (March 2026). A third adjusted home packages in May 2025 by simultaneously improving speeds and lowering prices, and scrapped long-term two- and three-year contracts to give customers more flexibility. What is clear across all operators is that these adjustments were not driven by improved efficiency — they were defensive moves made under financial pressure.

MNO Collective Response

In March 2026, BTC, Mascom, and Orange approached BOCRA collectively under the newly formed Botswana Telecommunications Operators Association. The association raised concerns about regulatory asymmetry — specifically that domestic operators comply with citizen employment requirements, invest in local infrastructure, and carry the cost of regulatory compliance, while Starlink operates with a fundamentally lighter regulatory burden.

BTC noted that subscriber losses have been most concentrated among residential and small business customers who previously relied on fixed wireless and FTTH services — the exact market segment Starlink has entered aggressively.

Common Operator Thread

Across all submissions, a consistent frustration emerged — not just at Starlink's competitive strength, but at the conditions under which it was licensed. Operators felt the playing field is not level, that the regulator has allowed Starlink to operate without meeting the same obligations as domestic licensees, and that regulatory inaction will have irreversible consequences for the local telecommunications industry.

MNO Resilience

Despite competitive disruption, the three MNOs have demonstrated remarkable revenue resilience. Orange peaked at BWP 486.7M in Q4 2025; Mascom achieved BWP 420.3M in the same period. Their urban customer base, bundled service offerings, and retail distribution networks provide structural protection not available to smaller ISPs.

Conclusion & Recommendations

Overall Market Assessment

Botswana's fixed broadband market is undergoing a structural transition. Starlink has introduced a new competitive dimension — satellite broadband — that complements terrestrial services in some segments and directly competes in others. The market is growing in volume but declining in diversity. The erosion of the independent ISP segment represents a structural risk to long-term competition, geographic coverage, and economic inclusivity.

Key Opportunities

Digital Inclusion Accelerator
Leverage Starlink's national coverage to advance broadband targets in Ghanzi, Kgalagadi, and Chobe where terrestrial build-out is economically unviable.
Digital Economy Levy & UASF
A 5% levy on satellite broadband revenues ring-fenced for a USAF could generate ~BWP 5.5M annually. Precedent in Nigeria, Rwanda, and Kenya.
ISP Niche Repositioning
Guide displaced ISPs toward enterprise LAN, municipal Wi-Fi, hospitality, and government managed services — verticals insulated from satellite competition.
Enterprise & Public Sector
Starlink's LEO broadband supports mining, agriculture, tourism, and public health. Structured institutional procurement programmes for schools and clinics.
Attract Competing Satellites
License Amazon Kuiper and Eutelsat OneWeb to prevent satellite monopoly formation and introduce pricing discipline in underserved segments.
Network Resilience
As a satellite-based system independent of terrestrial infrastructure, Starlink enhances national network resilience during fibre cuts, power outages, and natural disasters.
MNO Infrastructure Push
Satellite competition may compel Orange and Mascom to accelerate fibre and 5G FWA rollouts beyond urban areas — coverage advancement without public expenditure.
Regulatory Modernisation
Update licensing categories with technology-neutral NTN obligations. Revise spectrum pricing to ensure parity between terrestrial and satellite operators.
Tourism & Remote Economy
Eco-lodges and game ranches across national parks are prime LEO satellite users. Facilitate migration from legacy VSAT; formalise as a premium licence category.

Strategic Regulatory Recommendations

SAP Licence Renewal Conditionality — Due 13 May 2026
Make long-term licence renewal conditional on: settlement of all outstanding BOCRA turnover levies and UASF contributions; submission of audited financial statements; approval of a Botswana-specific data protection policy; and a binding local employment commitment above the current 1-person threshold.
Level Playing Field — Regulatory Obligations
Require Starlink to comply with the same citizen employment requirements, KYC obligations, local infrastructure investment mandates, and BINX peering requirements as all other licensed operators. Regulatory asymmetry is a market distortion that BOCRA must not permit to persist.
Protect Smaller ISPs — Emergency Monitoring Framework
At least one ISP is actively selling its business. Others have stopped all capital investment and are cutting staff. BOCRA should develop contingency frameworks for market exit scenarios before multiple exits occur simultaneously, to protect consumers and maintain geographic coverage.
Require Meaningful Local Presence
A company generating over BWP 27 million per quarter must be expected to contribute more meaningfully to the local economy. Minimum staffing thresholds, citizen employment targets, and local customer service centre requirements should be codified as binding licence conditions.
Data Protection Compliance — Data Protection Act 2024
Require a Botswana-tailored data protection policy, approved by the IDPC. Mandate demonstrable local storage of personal data collected from Botswana subscribers. Establish lawful interception compliance arrangements with clear points of contact for security authorities.
BINX Peering & Local Internet Ecosystem
Demand local interconnection and peering to support domestic internet ecosystem development including BINX. This is essential to prevent structural bypass of Botswana's internet exchange infrastructure and to reduce costs for all other operators routing local traffic.
MSS / VLEO Governance — Future Services
Ring-fence any Mobile Satellite Services (MSS) spectrum application or direct-to-phone offering as a separate authorisation, subject to full regulatory impact assessment. Starlink's VLEO launch could directly compete with MNO voice services — a governance framework must be in place before any such licence is granted.
Mandate Comprehensive Quarterly Reporting
Enforce complete revenue, subscription, employment, and CAPEX data submission from all licensed operators with proportionate penalties for non-compliance. Immediately address reporting gaps from Mascom (revenue), BTC (revenue), and Universal Wifi (all variables).
